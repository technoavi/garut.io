Does Indian schools need a Universal Learning Indicator.

  • Kuldeep Sinh Chavda
  • March 29, 2022

With nearly 15 lacs schools in India, we have the world’s 2nd largest schooling system in the world after China. Majority of the parents today choose private schools and hence 50% of the overall 25 Cr school students today, study in private schools making Indian private schooling system 3rd largest in the world. For following but obvious reasons, parents choose private schools-

   1. Quality of education/Better learning environments

   2. Preference for English Medium

But are these demand really met? If not, why? What are the key challenges faced by these schools? Why more than half of these schools end before board grade i.e. Xth? How can technology help our institutions and educators to drive better results?

Well, it will require more than one article to understand the above questions and possibilities that are available to address them. To begin with, lets understand what is a universal learning indicator and if India needs it.

Universal Learning Indicator is a standardized measure that helps parents compare learning performances across schools and make informed decision for enrollment. For example, in school ‘ABC’ 98% of grade 5 students can divide a 3-digit number by a single digit. 97% of grade 5 students read with comprehension.

According to Annual Status of Education Report 2019, more than 60% students of grade 5 cannot do division and more than 30% can’t read a grade 2 level paragraph. This report suggests that only 65.1% of grade 5 students can read grade 2 level paragraph and only 39.8% of grade 5 students can divide a 3-digit number by a single digit. Success rate for governments schools are much lower than this – 44.2% at reading and only 22.7% at arithmetic test.

It would improve in later years, you might wonder. But that’s not the case.

For many private school students, the failure to attain foundational skills are reflected in a standardized and curriculum-based exam they take in 10th grade, as seen in NAS. Students in grade 10 in private schools scored below 50% on an average in 4/5 subjects (NCERT 2017). Most affected in Mathematics and Science.

Problem is the information asymmetry and hence parents’ decision to choose schools are based on weak proxies like infrastructure or reputation. Since these are visible to parents, Schools also differentiate themselves by focusing only on improving proxies rather than actual learning. Their marketing focuses often on these proxies.

Having Universal Learning Indicator is nothing but to give parents the school reports on actual learning. This will then lead to schools compete on actual learning rather than proxies.

There is enough evidence on this being useful. Look out for

   1. Experiment in rural Ajmer by Indian Statistical Institute

   2. The LEAPS program (Harvard Evidence for Policy Design) in Punjab schools

   3. National School rating system in Chile (South America)

What we need is a regulation for learning improvement, something similar to National School Rating and also technology that really helps schools and educators implement different learning methodologies like flipped classroom approach.
